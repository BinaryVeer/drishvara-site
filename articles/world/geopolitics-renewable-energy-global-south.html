The Geopolitics of Renewable Energy Transitions in the Global South

Shifting alliances and strategies in emerging economies
This piece explores the challenges and opportunities faced by Global South countries as they navigate energy sovereignty, foreign investment, and climate commitments amid the shift to renewable energy.
Image credit: en:Gerard van Schagen · Source source_curated

As the global energy landscape undergoes a profound transformation, the Global South finds itself at the crossroads of opportunity and complexity. The shift towards renewable energy sources is not merely an environmental imperative but a geopolitical pivot that is reshaping alliances, economic dependencies, and development strategies across emerging economies.

For many countries in Africa, Latin America, and parts of Asia, renewable energy offers a pathway to greater energy sovereignty. Unlike traditional fossil fuel imports, renewables such as solar, wind, and hydropower can be harnessed locally, reducing reliance on volatile global markets and foreign energy suppliers. This potential for self-sufficiency is particularly appealing amid ongoing geopolitical tensions that have historically disrupted energy supplies and inflated costs.

However, the transition is not without its challenges. Financing renewable infrastructure often requires significant foreign investment, which can introduce new forms of dependency. Emerging economies must carefully balance the benefits of capital inflows with the risks of ceding control over critical assets or falling into debt traps. Moreover, technology transfer and capacity building remain uneven, with many countries still reliant on external expertise for installation, maintenance, and innovation in renewable technologies.

Geopolitically, the rise of renewables is prompting a realignment of global partnerships. Traditional energy exporters are investing in renewable projects abroad, while new actors—such as China and the European Union—are positioning themselves as key suppliers of green technology and financing. This dynamic is creating both competition and collaboration opportunities for Global South countries seeking to leverage their natural resources and strategic locations.

Development strategies are also evolving to integrate climate commitments with economic growth goals. Renewable energy projects are increasingly framed as vehicles for sustainable development, job creation, and improved energy access. Yet, governments must navigate complex trade-offs, ensuring that the shift to clean energy does not exacerbate social inequalities or environmental degradation in other forms.

Ultimately, the renewable energy transition in the Global South is a nuanced process shaped by local contexts and global forces. It demands thoughtful policy design, inclusive governance, and international cooperation that respects the sovereignty and aspirations of emerging economies. As these countries chart their energy futures, their experiences will be critical to understanding the broader geopolitical contours of a decarbonizing world.
